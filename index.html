Paris (1919-1938)

Mondrian was drawn to the abstract art of Paris and made it is home in 1911. Under the influence of Cubist artists like Picasso and Georges Braques, Mondrian’s art became more and more abstract.

In 1919, he began producing the works for which he would become most well-known. He used a Dutch term, Nieuwe Beelding, which meant “new forming,” which he later translated into French as Le Neo Plasticisme, or NEO-PLASTICISM in English.

The works were Mondrian’s attempt to deploy color and line without referencing external factors. Using these simple elements, Mondrian suggests lines and evocations of space.

Through his art, he sought to create an art that “expresses pure relationships.” He believed his art was manifesting a new spirit world coming into being.

London (1938-1940)

Fearing impending war and feeling the art scene in Paris was dying as a result of it, Mondrian once again picked up to move to London. Having sold paintings and contributed to British publications, he had a natural base in the UK already.

There are many works that Mondrian began in this period, but with fears of the war, most of them were finished in his years in New York and are dubbed the Transatlantic Works. The works are an evolution of his Neoplasticist works in Paris, with increasing amounts of white space in the compositions.

New York (1940-1944)

With war overtaking Europe, Mondrian fled London for New York, where he found his art newly invigorated by the so-called “boogie woogie” all around him in the city.

His compositions came to life, like the bustling streets of New York with line work that strayed from the typical black and an increased amount of squares that almost suggest the pedestrians on a city street.

Seminal works like “Broadway Boogie Woogie” only begin to suggest Mondrian’s new take on his city before his untimely death in 1944.
